## 4. Conversion Audit — Workshop Registration Page

**Short answer: yes, signing up is easy** — it's a 2-field form (first name + email), one button, no account creation, and a spam honeypot that's invisible to real visitors. The JS intercepts the submit so people get an instant "You're In! 🎉" confirmation without leaving the page or waiting on a redirect. That part is solid and shouldn't be touched.

Where it could leak registrations, especially from Facebook traffic landing cold:

1. **No replay/"can't make it live" safety net stated anywhere.** A huge share of your Facebook audience will be lurking on a Saturday afternoon — if they're not sure they'll catch it live, many just won't bother registering. State plainly on the page (and in the confirmation email from `workshop-register.js`) whether a recording goes out afterward. If yes, that single sentence — "Can't make it live? Register anyway and we'll send the replay" — will measurably lift sign-ups. (The pinned post above already promises this, so the page copy needs to match it.)

2. **No social proof on the page itself.** You have the Carol & Jim story sitting in your drip emails (and now in Draft E) — none of it is on `workshop.html`. A cold Facebook visitor who's never heard of you has nothing to anchor trust to beyond the host photo. Even one short pull-quote near the registration box would help.

3. **"Arizona Time" is genuinely confusing for a Facebook-wide audience.** Arizona doesn't observe daylight saving, so "AZ time" maps to different zones depending on season and where the visitor lives. Spell it out: "1:00 PM Arizona Time (12:00 PM Pacific / 3:00 PM Eastern)." This is a real friction point for anyone trying to mentally convert a time zone while half-distracted scrolling Facebook.

4. **No Open Graph tags (`og:title`, `og:description`, `og:image`).** This is the highest-priority fix given the channel: when any of the Facebook posts above get shared, liked, or commented-and-reshared, Facebook scrapes the page for a preview card. Without OG tags, that card will show up blank, ugly, or wrong — actively hurting the exact organic reach this whole plan depends on ("tag your spouse" posts live and die on whether the link preview looks inviting).

5. **No add-to-calendar link.** Facebook ads are great at getting the click and the registration, bad at getting people to actually remember a specific Saturday three weeks out. A "Add to Google Calendar" button on the thank-you state (and in the confirmation email) directly fights no-shows.

6. **The registration form doesn't capture traffic source.** Since this is a Facebook-first push, add a hidden field (e.g. `source=facebook`) or rely on a `?utm_source=facebook` query param so you can later see, in the Blobs/registrant data, how many actual sign-ups came from Facebook vs. anywhere else. Right now there's no way to know which post drove which registration.

**Priority order:** #4 (OG tags) first since it affects every single post in this plan, then #1 (replay policy — decide it, then state it everywhere), then #3 (time zone clarity) and #2 (social proof). #5 and #6 are real but lower-urgency polish.

## 5. Building an Email List From This Workshop (You Have None Yet)

Right now, every name + email collected by `workshop-register.js` gets saved to a Netlify Blobs store called `"workshop"` — that store **is** the seed of your first real email list. Treat the workshop as a list-building event, not just a one-off:

1. **Export the `workshop` Blobs store after registration closes** and load it into a proper email tool (Mailchimp, Kit/ConvertKit, etc.) — Blobs is fine for triggering automated sends (like the existing `drip.js` does for the guide), but you'll eventually want a real ESP for segmentation, reporting, and list hygiene as this grows.

2. **Gate the replay behind email, not just live attendance.** If you promise "can't make it live? we'll send the replay," that promise *is* the list-building mechanism — anyone who registers becomes a lead even if they never show up to the Zoom.

3. **Capture emails again, live, during the workshop.** On the Zoom call itself, ask attendees to drop their email in chat for "workshop notes + the replay link" — this catches anyone who joined via a shared link without registering on the page first (common with FB Lives / shared Zoom links).

4. **Immediately enroll every workshop registrant into a short follow-up sequence** — you already have the infrastructure (`drip.js` + the `subscribers` Blobs store from the guide funnel). Build a small 2-3 email "workshop attendee" variant: (1) thank-you + recap + replay link, (2) invite to the free Skool community, (3) soft invite to the Reignite program — same warm, no-pressure voice as the existing welcome sequence.

5. **Use the free Skool community as your backup list.** It's already linked everywhere in your emails — push it hard at the end of the workshop too ("come keep this conversation going"). A community you own is insulated from any future Facebook algorithm or policy changes in a way your FB followers aren't.

6. **Every Facebook post should ultimately funnel through the registration form**, even the pure-engagement ones (Draft C, Draft F) — comments and tags are great for reach, but the form is the only place an actual lead gets captured. Don't let an engagement post be the end of the funnel; always circle back to "register below."
